# NBDHE Chapter Study Guides
## What You Really Need to Know — By Chapter

---

## Cap. 2 – Histología y Embriología (101 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Not cell biology — dental structures specifically.

### 7 Pillars

1. **Tooth development stages** — dental lamina → bud → cap → bell → crown; what goes wrong at each (gemination, fusion, dens in dente, amelogenesis imperfecta)
2. **Who makes what** — ameloblasts (enamel, die after eruption), odontoblasts (dentin, live in pulp forever), cementoblasts (cementum), fibroblasts (PDL)
3. **Enamel** — hardest body substance, no cells after eruption, cannot regenerate, Hunter-Schreger bands, enamel rods perpendicular to DEJ
4. **Dentin** — dentinal tubules = sensitivity pathway, predentin = new dentin forming, tertiary dentin = injury response, widest at pulp
5. **Pulp** — Weil's cell-free zone, odontoblastic layer at outer edge, secondary dentin narrows chamber with age
6. **PDL fibers** — alveolar crest, horizontal, oblique (most numerous), apical, interradicular; Sharpey's fibers embed into cementum and bone
7. **Oral mucosa types** — masticatory (keratinized: gingiva + hard palate), lining (non-keratinized: cheeks, floor, lips), specialized (tongue dorsum, taste buds)

### Teaching Idea
Draw a blank tooth cross-section — students label every structure and name the cell responsible. TTS: "Name the cell that forms enamel. Can it repair enamel after eruption?" Drills the most-tested fact in this chapter.

## Cap. 3 – Anatomía y Fisiología (60 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Cell biology and organ systems as they explain dental disease and patient management.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Cell organelles and function** — mitochondria (energy/ATP), ribosomes (protein synthesis), ER (lipids/carbohydrates), nucleus (DNA/RNA) — these show up as direct recall questions
2. **Cellular respiration** — glycolysis → pyruvate → acetyl-CoA → Krebs cycle → electron transport chain; anaerobic bacteria stop at lactic acid (relevant to caries)
3. **Tissue types** — epithelial (covering/lining), connective (support), muscle (cardiac/skeletal/smooth), nerve — know what each does and where it lives
4. **Cardiovascular system** — heart chambers and valves in order, normal BP ranges, pulse points, what compromised cardiac function means for patient management
5. **Endocrine system** — insulin/glucagon (diabetes implications), thyroid (hypo vs hyperthyroid presentation), adrenal (steroid use → adrenal crisis risk)
6. **Nervous system** — somatic vs autonomic (sympathetic "fight or flight" vs parasympathetic "rest and digest"); why epinephrine in anesthesia has cardiovascular effects

### Teaching Idea
Organ system rapid-fire — instructor names a system, students name one dental implication. ("Endocrine" → "Diabetic patients have impaired healing.") TTS version reads a patient's medical history; students identify which body system is relevant.

## Cap. 4 – Anatomía de Cabeza y Cuello (50 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** What's in the head and neck that a hygienist might palpate, inject near, or find pathology in.

### 7 Pillars

1. **Directional terminology** — superior/inferior, anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, buccal/lingual, apical/coronal, proximal/distal — tested directly as vocabulary
2. **Bones of skull and face** — mandible (condyle, coronoid, ramus, angle, body), maxilla, zygomatic, temporal, frontal, parietal, occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid
3. **Muscles of mastication** — masseter (elevation), temporalis (elevation + retraction), medial pterygoid (elevation), lateral pterygoid (depression + protrusion) — tested by function
4. **Salivary glands** — parotid (Stensen's duct, opens opposite upper 2nd molar), submandibular (Wharton's duct, opens at lingual frenum), sublingual (Bartholin's duct)
5. **Trigeminal nerve (CN V)** — V1 ophthalmic, V2 maxillary (PSA, MSA, ASA, infraorbital, nasopalatine, GP), V3 mandibular (IAN, lingual, buccal, mental) — critical for anesthesia
6. **Lymph nodes** — submental, submandibular, cervical chains; know which drain which oral structures — relevant for cancer assessment
7. **Blood supply** — external carotid → facial, lingual, maxillary arteries; internal maxillary → superior/inferior alveolar

### Teaching Idea
Palpation drill — partner palpates lymph nodes while explaining what they drain. Role-play: "You feel a fixed, hard node under the angle of the mandible. What do you do next?" TTS: describe a location, students name which nerve or gland is there.

## Cap. 5 – Anatomía Dental (49 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Root morphology (for instrumentation), eruption dates, and occlusion basics.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Numbering systems** — Universal (1–32 permanent, A–T primary), Palmer (quadrant grid), FDI/ISO (two-digit); conversion questions appear on the exam
2. **Eruption sequence** — primary (central incisors first ~6 months, 2nd molars last ~2 years) vs permanent (1st molars + central incisors ~6 years, 3rd molars ~17–21 years)
3. **Root anatomy by tooth** — incisors: single, shortest; maxillary 1st premolar: bifurcated (most variable); maxillary molars: 3 roots (MB, DB, P); mandibular molars: 2 roots (M, D); longest root = maxillary canine
4. **Crown landmarks** — cusps, ridges (marginal, triangular, transverse, oblique), grooves (developmental vs supplemental), fossae, pits
5. **CEJ and contact areas** — CEJ = scalloped on facial, flatter on proximal; contacts located at junction of occlusal and middle thirds for posteriors
6. **Occlusion** — Angle's Class I (mesiobuccal cusp of max 1st molar in buccal groove of mand 1st molar), Class II (distal), Class III (mesial); overbite vs overjet; centric vs eccentric occlusion

### Teaching Idea
Tooth ID tray — extracted teeth or models, students identify from morphology alone. TTS: "This tooth has three roots, the palatal being the longest and straightest. What tooth is it?" Root anatomy is the most clinically tested because it directly affects instrumentation decisions.

## Cap. 6 – Radiología (53 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Radiation safety, exposure errors (and how to fix them), and reading what's normal vs abnormal on a film.

### 7 Pillars

1. **Radiation physics** — X-rays are ionizing electromagnetic radiation; produced when electrons decelerate hitting tungsten target (Bremsstrahlung) or eject inner-shell electrons (characteristic)
2. **Exposure factors** — kVp controls contrast (higher kVp = lower contrast = more gray tones); mA controls density/darkness; distance follows inverse square law
3. **Radiation biology** — ionization damages DNA; stochastic effects (cancer, genetic) have no threshold; deterministic effects (cataract, erythema) have a threshold; fetus most radiosensitive
4. **Radiation protection (ALARA)** — lead apron + thyroid collar on every patient; rectangular collimation (reduces dose 60%); fastest film speed (F) or digital; no finger-holding
5. **Technique errors and corrections:**
   - Elongation → increase vertical angulation (or film tilted toward lingual)
   - Foreshortening → decrease vertical angulation
   - Cone-cut → reposition cone
   - Overlapping → correct horizontal angulation
6. **Normal anatomical landmarks** — maxillary: incisive foramen, nasal septum, maxillary sinus, zygomatic arch, hamular notch; mandibular: mental foramen, mandibular canal, external oblique ridge, genial tubercles
7. **Radiographic interpretation** — caries = radiolucent; calculus/bone/intact enamel = radiopaque; bone loss: horizontal (parallel to CEJ) vs vertical (angular); periapical abscess vs cyst vs granuloma

### Teaching Idea
Error identification drill — show a film with one error, students name the error and state the correction. TTS: "The patient's image shows elongated teeth. What did the operator do wrong, and how do you fix it?" Role-play full radiation protection compliance from start to finish.

## Cap. 7 – Patología General (30 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** The disease process of dental caries and basic inflammatory/immune concepts that underpin all dental disease.

### 5 Pillars

1. **Inflammation cascade** — acute (rapid, neutrophils, cardinal signs: rubor, calor, tumor, dolor, functio laesa) vs chronic (slow, macrophages + lymphocytes, tissue destruction + repair cycle)
2. **Caries as a disease** — Stephan curve (pH drops below 5.5 critical threshold), S. mutans initiates, Lactobacillus progresses; smooth surface vs pit-and-fissure vs root caries differ in location and causative bacteria
3. **Healing and repair** — primary intention (clean wound edges) vs secondary intention (gap, granulation tissue forms); factors impairing healing: diabetes, immunosuppression, smoking, malnutrition, dry socket
4. **Neoplasia basics** — benign (encapsulated, slow, doesn't metastasize) vs malignant (invasive, rapid, metastasizes); carcinoma (epithelial) vs sarcoma (connective tissue); SCC is most common oral malignancy
5. **Cell injury and death** — necrosis (pathological, inflammatory response), apoptosis (programmed, no inflammation); atrophy (decrease in size), hypertrophy (increase in size), hyperplasia (increase in number)

### Teaching Idea
Caries scenario — "pH in this patient's mouth drops to 5.2 after eating. Enamel demineralization begins at what pH? What bacteria is primarily responsible?" TTS cases built around the Stephan curve work extremely well here.

## Cap. 8 – Patología Oral (86 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Recognize a lesion by its description, know whether it's benign or dangerous, and know what to do next.

### 7 Pillars

1. **Lesion terminology** — macule (flat color change), papule (raised <5mm), nodule (raised >5mm), vesicle (fluid-filled <5mm), bulla (fluid-filled >5mm), pustule (pus), ulcer (loss of epithelium), erosion, plaque
2. **White lesions** — leukoplakia (premalignant, cannot be wiped off), candidiasis (white, CAN be wiped off, pseudomembranous type), lichen planus (Wickham's striae, bilateral), linea alba (benign, occlusal line)
3. **Oral cancer** — SCC = >90% of oral cancers; most common site = lateral/ventral tongue + floor of mouth; risk factors: tobacco + alcohol (synergistic), HPV-16 (oropharyngeal), UV (lip); red lesions more dangerous than white
4. **Ulcerative lesions** — aphthous (minor/major/herpetiform, recurrent, not contagious), primary herpes (HSV-1, systemic symptoms, highly contagious, vesicles that rupture), recurrent herpes (localized, lip/hard palate only)
5. **Systemic diseases with oral manifestations** — diabetes (xerostomia, impaired healing, increased periodontitis), HIV (oral candidiasis, hairy leukoplakia, Kaposi's sarcoma, NUG/NUP), sickle cell (stepladder trabeculation on X-ray)
6. **Benign lesions** — fibroma (most common oral tumor, from trauma), pyogenic granuloma (pregnancy tumor, highly vascular), torus palatinus/mandibularis (normal bony variant), mucocele (lower lip, saliva retention)
7. **Jaw cysts** — periapical cyst (at apex of non-vital tooth), dentigerous cyst (around crown of unerupted tooth), OKC/KCOT (aggressive, high recurrence, associated with Gorlin syndrome)

### Teaching Idea
Lesion description game — read a clinical description, students diagnose. "A 55-year-old smoker presents with a non-tender, non-wipeable white plaque on the lateral tongue present for 2 months. What is it and what do you do?" These scenarios mirror exactly how the exam tests this chapter.

## Cap. 9 – Microbiología e Inmunología (49 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Which bacteria cause which diseases, how infection control stops them, and how the immune system responds.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Bacterial classification** — gram positive (thick peptidoglycan, purple stain: Streptococcus, Staphylococcus) vs gram negative (thin wall + LPS outer membrane, pink stain: most periodontal pathogens); aerobic vs anaerobic
2. **Periodontal pathogens** — Red complex = P. gingivalis, T. forsythia, T. denticola (severe periodontitis); Orange complex = F. nucleatum (bridge organism); A. actinomycetemcomitans (aggressive periodontitis in young patients)
3. **Cariogenic bacteria** — S. mutans (colonizes first, acid production, early caries), Lactobacillus (thrives in acidic environment, drives progression), S. sobrinus (secondary)
4. **Sterilization vs disinfection** — sterilization kills ALL including spores (autoclave 121°C/15psi, dry heat 160°C); high-level disinfection kills all except spores (glutaraldehyde); intermediate kills most (isopropyl alcohol); low-level (soap)
5. **Immune system** — innate (immediate, non-specific: neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells) vs adaptive (specific: B-cells → antibodies, T-cells → cell-mediated); IgA = secretory/saliva; IgG = most common serum; IgM = first response; IgE = allergy
6. **Relevant viruses** — HSV-1 (oral herpes, latent in trigeminal ganglion), HBV + HCV (bloodborne, dental concern; HBV vaccine required), HIV (retrovirus, CD4+ T-cell target), HPV-16 (oropharyngeal cancer)

### Teaching Idea
Red/orange/green complex card drill — bacteria name on one side, complex and associated disease on the other. TTS: "A 25-year-old has severe bone loss not explained by local factors. Which bacterium is associated with this presentation?" Role-play: walk through sterilization protocol from dirty instrument to sterile — identify where cross-contamination could occur.

## Cap. 11 – Farmacología (43 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Drug interactions and contraindications that directly affect patient safety in the dental chair.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Local anesthetics** — mechanism: blocks Na+ channels, stopping nerve depolarization; esters (procaine, benzocaine — metabolized in plasma, higher allergy rate) vs amides (lidocaine, articaine, mepivacaine — metabolized in liver, safer); max doses: lidocaine 2% = 4.4 mg/kg (300mg max adult)
2. **Vasoconstrictors** — epinephrine prolongs anesthesia + reduces systemic absorption + hemostasis; 1:100,000 for most; 1:200,000 for cardiac-compromised; avoid with MAOIs (hypertensive crisis) and non-selective beta-blockers
3. **Analgesics** — NSAIDs (ibuprofen: anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, analgesic; caution: peptic ulcer, renal disease, blood thinners); acetaminophen (safe for GI/renal but hepatotoxic — liver disease or alcohol use); opioids = last resort
4. **Antibiotics** — amoxicillin (first line for dental infections); metronidazole (anaerobes, periodontal pathogens); clindamycin (penicillin allergy alternative); doxycycline (periodontal adjunct); tetracycline contraindicated in children < 8 years (tooth staining)
5. **Drug interactions to know** — warfarin + NSAIDs = bleeding risk; alcohol + acetaminophen = liver toxicity; epinephrine + MAOIs = hypertensive crisis; bisphosphonates = MRONJ risk with invasive procedures
6. **Drugs causing oral side effects** — xerostomia (antihistamines, antidepressants, antihypertensives); gingival hyperplasia (phenytoin, cyclosporine, nifedipine); staining (tetracycline, chlorhexidine long-term); altered taste (metronidazole, ACE inhibitors)

### Teaching Idea
Drug card flip drill — drug name on front, class/mechanism/one key contraindication on back. TTS scenario: "Your patient takes warfarin and has a dental infection. The dentist wants to prescribe ibuprofen. What's the problem and what's the safer choice?" These pharmacology scenarios are exactly the style used on the NBDHE.

## Cap. 12 – Bioquímica y Nutrición (47 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Which vitamin/mineral deficiency causes which oral finding, and how diet drives caries.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Vitamins with oral manifestations:**
   - Vitamin C deficiency → scurvy: swollen, bleeding, spongy gingiva, loose teeth
   - Vitamin D + calcium deficiency → rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults), delayed eruption, hypocalcified enamel
   - B2 (riboflavin) deficiency → glossitis, cheilosis, angular cheilitis
   - B12/folate deficiency → atrophic glossitis (smooth, red/painful tongue), macrocytic anemia
   - Vitamin A deficiency → xerophthalmia, impaired wound healing; excess → hepatotoxic
   - Vitamin K → necessary for clotting factors II, VII, IX, X; deficiency = bleeding risk
2. **Minerals** — calcium + phosphorus (teeth and bone), fluoride (replaces OH in hydroxyapatite → fluorapatite, more acid-resistant), iron deficiency → angular cheilitis + glossitis + Plummer-Vinson syndrome
3. **Cariogenic potential of diet** — fermentable carbohydrates = substrate for S. mutans; frequency of exposure more damaging than total amount; Stephan curve pH drops below 5.5; sticky carbs (raisins, crackers) linger longer than liquid sugars
4. **Protective foods** — cheese (raises pH, provides calcium), xylitol (non-fermentable, inhibits S. mutans), water (dilutes, rinses), fluoridated water
5. **Fat-soluble vs water-soluble vitamins** — A, D, E, K = fat-soluble, stored (toxicity possible with excess); B-complex + C = water-soluble, excreted (deficiency more common, toxicity rare except B6)
6. **Fluoride mechanism** — systemic fluoride incorporates during tooth formation; topical fluoride remineralizes early enamel lesions; optimal water fluoride = 0.7 ppm

### Teaching Idea
Deficiency-to-lesion matching drill — symptom described, student names the missing nutrient. TTS: "A patient has a smooth, red, painful tongue and reports fatigue and tingling in her hands. What deficiency do you suspect?" Diet diary role-play — patient reports their daily diet, student identifies cariogenic patterns and makes a specific recommendation.

## Cap. 13 – Biomateriales (100 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Which material to use when, and more importantly, which material NOT to use and why.

### Critical Contraindication Table

| Material | Critical Rule |
|---|---|
| ZOE | Eugenol inhibits composite polymerization — never use as base under resin |
| APF fluoride | Etches ceramic/composite/porcelain — use NaF instead |
| Amalgam | High-copper eliminates gamma-2 phase (corrosion + creep) |
| Glass ionomer | Only material that bonds chemically to tooth; releases fluoride |
| Alginate | Must pour immediately or wrap in wet gauze; tears if too dry |
| PVS/polyvinyl siloxane | Most accurate impression; latex inhibits set — no latex gloves |
| Phosphoric acid etch | 35–37%, 15–30 sec; must be rinsed completely; dry = frosty white |
| Composite | Polymerization shrinkage toward light source; needs bonding agent |
| Zinc phosphate | Strongest luting cement; exothermic mix (cool glass slab); no fluoride |

### 5 Conceptual Pillars

1. **Impression materials** — reversible vs irreversible hydrocolloids; elastomeric (PVS most accurate, polyether very stiff, polysulfide longest working time)
2. **Gypsum products** — plaster (type II) < stone (type III) < die stone (type IV) in strength; higher type = less water in mix (W:P ratio decreases)
3. **Cements and bases** — thermal insulation (protect pulp from temperature), sedative (ZOE calms pulp), luting (holds restoration), liner (thin layer)
4. **Fluoride delivery forms** — APF gel 1.23% (4 min, contraindicated on ceramics), NaF varnish 5% (most effective, safe on all surfaces), SnF2 gel (antibacterial but stains)
5. **Whitening** — carbamide peroxide breaks down to hydrogen peroxide + urea; lower concentration = longer wear time; sensitivity is main side effect; active caries must be treated first

### Teaching Idea
"Can I use this?" scenario drill. "The patient has porcelain veneers and you're about to apply fluoride. Which product do you reach for and why?" Since your candidates are already clinically experienced, frame these as chair-side decision points they've encountered but may not have known the scientific reason behind.

## Cap. 14 – Periodoncia (63 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Classification of disease, reading a perio chart, and knowing what drives treatment decisions.

### 7 Pillars

1. **Periodontal anatomy** — free gingiva (sulcus 1–3mm), attached gingiva (keratinized, stippled when healthy), interdental papilla, mucogingival junction, alveolar mucosa; biologic width = ~2mm (junctional epithelium + connective tissue attachment)
2. **AAP 2017 Classification** — Staging (I–IV) by severity: probing depths, bone loss, tooth loss; Grading (A–C) by rate of progression: A = slow (no risk factors), B = moderate, C = rapid (smoker, diabetic)
3. **Gingivitis vs periodontitis** — gingivitis: inflammation, no bone loss, reversible; periodontitis: bone loss + CAL, not fully reversible; key threshold = attachment loss
4. **Bone loss patterns** — horizontal (equal loss on adjacent teeth = most common) vs vertical/angular (unequal loss, creates infrabony pockets); furcation involvement: Class I (probe doesn't enter), II (enters, doesn't go through), III (through-and-through)
5. **Indices** — Gingival Index (0–3, bleeding only), Plaque Index (0–3), Bleeding on Probing (yes/no per site), PSR/CPI (screening tool), OHI-S (debris + calculus)
6. **Systemic connections** — diabetes (bidirectional: periodontitis worsens glycemic control); cardiovascular disease (shared bacteria + inflammatory markers); pregnancy (preterm/low birth weight association); smoking (masks BOP, impairs healing, worst risk factor for progression)
7. **Treatment sequence** — emergency → Phase I (SRP, OHI, risk factor control) → re-eval at 4–6 weeks → Phase II (surgery if needed) → Phase III (maintenance every 3–4 months for periodontitis patients)

### Teaching Idea
Perio chart reading drill — hand a filled-in chart, students must identify: staging, grading, which teeth have furcation involvement, which areas to prioritize. TTS: "Probing depths 5–6mm, BOP present, 2mm CAL, non-smoker, well-controlled type 2 diabetes. Stage and grade this patient." Role-play the re-evaluation conversation — explaining to a patient why they need 3-month recall instead of 6.

## Cap. 15 – Cuidado de Higiene Dental (50 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** The ADPIE process and clinical decision-making at every patient appointment.

### 6 Pillars

1. **ADPIE process** — Assessment (data collection) → Diagnosis (dental hygiene problem + etiology) → Planning (goals, interventions, sequence) → Implementation (executing care) → Evaluation (did it work? what's next?)
2. **Vital signs and what they mean:**
   - BP: Normal <120/80; Elevated 120–129/<80; Stage 1 HTN 130–139/80–89; Stage 2 ≥140/90; Crisis >180/120 → defer elective treatment, refer
   - Pulse: normal 60–100; rate, rhythm, volume all assessed
   - Respiration: 12–20/min; Temperature: 98.6°F
3. **Health history red flags** — anticoagulants (bleeding time), bisphosphonates (MRONJ risk), immunosuppressants (infection risk), cardiac conditions (premedication, stress reduction), pregnancy (no supine in 3rd trimester, no elective X-rays in 1st)
4. **Medical consult indications** — uncontrolled hypertension, uncontrolled diabetes, bleeding disorders, severe cardiac disease, recent MI (wait 6 months), recent stroke (wait 6 months)
5. **Treatment sequence** — emergency care → etiologic phase (SRP, OHI) → re-evaluation → definitive care (restorations, surgery) → maintenance
6. **Patient communication** — health literacy assessment; motivational interviewing (open-ended questions, reflective listening, avoid confrontation); teach-back method to confirm understanding

### Teaching Idea
Full patient scenario role-play — one person plays a patient with complex medical history (hypertension + warfarin + possible periodontal disease), another plays the hygienist and must take the health history, identify red flags, and explain the plan. Best chapter for long-form clinical simulation.

## Cap. 16 – Prevención de Enfermedades (41 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Fluoride dosing, sealant indications, and the public health prevention model.

### 5 Pillars

1. **Levels of prevention** — primary (prevent disease before it starts: fluoride, sealants, OHI), secondary (early detection and treatment: radiographic caries detection, early SRP), tertiary (limit damage after disease: restoration, prosthetics, implants)
2. **Fluoride dosing by delivery:**
   - Water: 0.7 ppm optimal; >2 ppm → fluorosis risk
   - Supplements: only if water <0.6 ppm; 0–3 years: 0mg, 3–6 years: 0.25mg, 6–16 years: 0.5–1mg
   - Varnish (5% NaF): safest, best for children and patients with ceramics
   - APF gel (1.23%): 4-minute tray, not for ceramics/composites
   - Rx high-dose toothpaste (5000 ppm NaF): for high-caries-risk adults
3. **Sealants** — indicated for pit-and-fissure surfaces of permanent molars in high-risk children; acid etch technique; resin-based preferred; glass ionomer as interim for children who can't cooperate; can seal over incipient caries (arrest progression)
4. **Oral hygiene instruction** — Bass/sulcular brushing technique = standard (45° angle at gumline, short strokes); flossing before brushing; interdental brushes for furcations/spaces; chlorhexidine rinse (0.12%) for short-term adjunct therapy — caution: staining with long-term use
5. **Tobacco cessation (5 A's)** — Ask (screen every patient), Advise (clear strong advice), Assess (readiness to quit), Assist (resources, NRT), Arrange (follow-up); pharmacotherapy: nicotine replacement therapy (patch, gum, lozenge), varenicline, bupropion

### Teaching Idea
Fluoride decision tree — present a patient profile (age, water fluoride level, caries risk, restorations), students work through which fluoride product is appropriate. TTS: "A 7-year-old in an area with 0.3 ppm fluoride in water. What supplement dose do you recommend?" Tobacco cessation role-play with an actor playing a defensive patient who doesn't want to quit — practice motivational interviewing under pressure.

## Cap. 17 – Instrumentación y Evaluación (11 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Understanding instrument design and selection, not memorizing names. Largely tested through patient cases.

### 5 Pillars

1. **Instrument anatomy** — handle, shank, working end (blade); terminal shank direction tells you which area the instrument is designed for
2. **Universal vs Gracey curettes** — Universal: both cutting edges, used on all surfaces; Gracey: one cutting edge, area-specific (1/2 anterior, 11/12 posteriors mesial, 13/14 posteriors distal)
3. **Adaptation** — leading third of the blade against the tooth; toe-third for tight pockets
4. **Angulation** — 0–5° for exploring (light, no pressure), 45–90° for scaling/root planing (optimal ≈ 70°)
5. **Stroke types** — exploratory (assessment, light pressure), scaling (dislodge calculus, lateral pressure), root planing (smooth root surface, overlapping strokes)

### Teaching Idea
TTS audio drill: "You're treating the mesio-buccal of #14. Which Gracey do you reach for?" Student answers before the reveal. In-person: actual instruments on a table, instructor calls a scenario, student selects the correct one. Muscle memory beats memorization here.

## Cap. 18 – Dolor y Ansiedad (50 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Which injection anesthetizes what, maximum doses, and nitrous oxide contraindications.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Mandibular injections** — IAN block (inferior alveolar nerve): all mandibular teeth + lip + chin on that side; Long Buccal: buccal gingiva of molars (separate injection); Mental: premolars + anterior lip; Gow-Gates: entire mandibular division
2. **Maxillary injections** — PSA: max molars except MB root of 1st; MSA: premolars + MB root of 1st; ASA: incisors + canine; Infraorbital: same as ASA + premolars; Nasopalatine: palatal tissue anterior 6; Greater Palatine: palatal tissue posteriors
3. **Vasoconstrictors** — 1:100,000 epinephrine (most procedures), 1:200,000 (cardiac-sensitive patients), 1:50,000 (hemostasis); max epi = 0.2mg per appointment for healthy adult; contraindicated: MAOIs, non-selective beta-blockers, uncontrolled hyperthyroidism
4. **Max local anesthetic doses:**
   - Lidocaine 2%: 4.4 mg/kg, max 300 mg (~8.3 carpules)
   - Articaine 4%: 7 mg/kg, max 500 mg
   - Mepivacaine 3% (no vasoconstrictor): 4.4 mg/kg, max 300 mg
5. **Nitrous oxide** — sedation, not analgesia alone; titrate to effect; post-op 100% O2 for 5 min (diffusion hypoxia); contraindicated: COPD, first trimester pregnancy, recent ear/sinus surgery, B12 deficiency, severe psychiatric conditions
6. **LA toxicity signs** — early CNS excitation: metallic taste, lightheadedness, tinnitus, circumoral numbness; then CNS depression → seizure → cardiovascular collapse; treatment: stop injection, O2, supportive care, call EMS if severe

### Teaching Idea
Injection sequence drill — given a procedure ("full-mouth debridement, maxillary right quadrant"), students list which injections are needed and in what order. TTS: "Your patient begins to report ringing in her ears and feels dizzy 2 minutes into the injection. What's happening and what do you do immediately?" Clinical mannequin simulation is ideal for this chapter.

## Cap. 19 – Necesidades Especiales (50 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** How to modify care for patients who don't fit the standard model — and when to consult or defer.

### 6 Pillars

1. **Pediatric patients** — behavior management (tell-show-do is first line; voice control; positive reinforcement); fluoride supplementation only if water <0.6 ppm; primary tooth anatomy differences (thinner enamel/dentin, larger relative pulp size, less calcified)
2. **Elderly patients** — polypharmacy (xerostomia from antihistamines, antidepressants, antihypertensives → root caries risk); root caries most common in this population; cognitive decline affects consent capacity
3. **Pregnant patients** — second trimester = safest window for elective treatment; semi-supine position after 20 weeks (prevents aortocaval compression); local anesthesia safe (Category B); no tetracycline; no elective X-rays in 1st trimester; pregnancy granuloma (pyogenic granuloma) common
4. **Antibiotic prophylaxis (AHA guidelines)** — indicated for: prosthetic heart valves, previous infective endocarditis, certain congenital heart conditions, cardiac transplant with valvulopathy; regimen: amoxicillin 2g PO 30–60 min before; clindamycin 600mg if PCN allergy; joint replacements generally no longer require prophylaxis
5. **Medically compromised:**
   - Diabetes: schedule morning appointments; have glucose source available; uncontrolled = impaired healing, more severe periodontitis, increased infection risk
   - Anticoagulants/bleeding disorders: consult physician; do not routinely discontinue warfarin (thromboembolism risk > local bleeding risk)
   - Immunocompromised: watch for opportunistic infections, delayed healing, increased caries
6. **Physical/developmental disabilities** — adapted OHI for caregivers; mouth prop use; power toothbrush often better; shorter appointments; communication adaptations for autism, cognitive impairment, hearing loss

### Teaching Idea
"Who is this patient?" role-play — actor plays an elderly patient on 12 medications, a pregnant patient in her third trimester, or a patient with uncontrolled diabetes. Hygienist must adapt the entire appointment. TTS: read a health history, students identify every modification needed before starting treatment.

## Cap. 20 – Salud Comunitaria (32 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Dental indices, research design basics, and water fluoridation facts.

### 5 Pillars

1. **Dental indices — know each one's purpose:**
   - DMFT/DMFS: decay, missing, filled teeth/surfaces — permanent dentition, irreversible
   - dmft/dmfs: same for primary dentition
   - OHI-S: debris index + calculus index; 0 = excellent, 6 = worst
   - GI (Löe & Silness): gingival inflammation 0–3, based on bleeding
   - Dean's Fluorosis Index: very mild → severe; 0.6 = community fluorosis concern threshold
   - CPI/PSR: screening tool only, not for detailed diagnosis
2. **Epidemiology terms** — incidence (new cases in a time period) vs prevalence (all existing cases at one point in time); incidence = new cases / population at risk; prevalence = incidence × duration
3. **Research design** — cross-sectional (snapshot, shows prevalence), longitudinal/cohort (follow over time, shows incidence), case-control (look backwards from disease), RCT (gold standard — random assignment, double-blind)
4. **Statistics basics** — null hypothesis (assumes no difference); Type I error (false positive — reject null when it's true); Type II error (false negative — fail to detect real difference); p <0.05 = statistically significant; mean, median, mode
5. **Water fluoridation** — optimal level 0.7 ppm; reduces caries 25–40% in children; fluorosis risk above 2 ppm; most cost-effective public health intervention in dentistry; CDC top 10 public health achievement of 20th century

### Teaching Idea
Index calculation drill — given a charted patient with tooth status recorded, calculate DMFT. Statistics scenario: "A study finds chlorhexidine rinse reduces plaque with p = 0.03. What does this mean, and what type of error are we protecting against?" Ground every concept in "what this tells you about a population."

## Cap. 21 – Emergencias (2 questions — needs more)

**What the exam really tests:** The correct first action and drug for each emergency. Sequence matters.

### The 6 Protocols

| Emergency | Position | First Action | Drug |
|---|---|---|---|
| Syncope | Trendelenburg (legs up) | Ammonia inhalant, monitor vitals | None typically |
| Angina | Upright | Stop treatment, rest, O2 | Nitroglycerin SL; if no relief in 5 min → treat as MI |
| Myocardial infarction | Supine | Call 911, O2 | Aspirin 325mg chewable |
| Anaphylaxis | Supine, legs up | Epinephrine IM (thigh) | 0.3mg epinephrine; call 911 |
| Hypoglycemia | Upright (if conscious) | Give sugar (OJ, glucose tabs) | Glucagon if unconscious; call 911 |
| Seizure | Chair flat, clear area | Protect head, do NOT restrain, time it | Nothing; recovery position after; call 911 if >5 min |

### Teaching Idea
This is the best chapter for live role-play. One student plays the patient (slumps, grabs chest, starts shaking), another plays the hygienist. TTS version: "You hear a crash from the waiting room. The patient is on the floor, unresponsive, not breathing. Call it." Timer running. Debrief after — the protocol discussion is the real lesson.

## Cap. 22 – Ética y Aspectos Legales (8 questions)

**What the exam really tests:** Applying ethical principles to clinical scenarios and knowing the legal vocabulary.

### The 12 Terms to Know Cold

| Term | One-Line Definition |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Patient's right to make their own decisions |
| Beneficence | Act in the patient's best interest |
| Non-maleficence | Do no harm |
| Justice | Fair treatment for all patients |
| Veracity | Tell the truth |
| Informed consent | Patient agrees after understanding risks, benefits, and alternatives |
| Implied consent | Assumed in emergencies when patient cannot consent |
| Confidentiality / HIPAA | Cannot share PHI without patient consent |
| Standard of care | What a reasonably prudent provider would do in the same situation |
| Negligence | Deviation from standard of care that causes harm |
| Abandonment | Ending the provider-patient relationship without proper notice |
| Scope of practice | What state law permits an RDH to do |

### Teaching Idea
Flashcard-style TTS — hear the definition, say the term. Or flip it: hear the term, explain it in one sentence. Role-play the hard ones: abandonment scenario (patient no-shows 3 times, hygienist wants to dismiss them — how?), informed consent refusal (patient declines X-rays — what do you document?).
