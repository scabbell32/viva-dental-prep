# Teaching Methods Guide
## Pedagogical Approaches by Chapter and Method Type

---

## Role Play / Skits / Scenes

Best for chapters where something **happens** — there's a before, a during, and an after, and a real human is either in distress, making a decision, or being persuaded.

| Chapter | Scene Ideas |
|---|---|
| **Cap. 21 – Emergencias** | Patient collapses. Protocol runs in real time. Every emergency is its own scene with a clock running. |
| **Cap. 22 – Ética** | Patient refuses treatment. Hygienist wants to dismiss a no-show patient. A colleague is doing something questionable. Real conversations with right and wrong answers and emotional stakes. |
| **Cap. 19 – Necesidades Especiales** | The appointment changes based on the patient. Elderly patient on 12 meds. Pregnant patient refusing X-rays. Patient with autism who is overwhelmed. Playing the patient teaches as much as playing the provider. |
| **Cap. 15 – Cuidado de Higiene Dental** | The full intake appointment. Health history, red flags, explaining findings, getting consent, delivering bad news ("you have periodontal disease"). Most complete patient interaction. |
| **Cap. 16 – Prevención** | Tobacco cessation using the 5 A's. Patient is defensive and doesn't want to hear it. That tension is exactly what the exam tests — motivational interviewing under pressure. |
| **Cap. 18 – Dolor y Ansiedad** | Anxious patient. Patient says anesthesia isn't working. Early signs of LA toxicity appearing mid-treatment. High-stakes real-time responses. |

## Flashcards

Best for chapters with **discrete, countable facts** — associations, names, numbers, definitions — where there's no process to understand, just items to retrieve.

| Chapter | What to Drill |
|---|---|
| **Cap. 22 – Ética** | 12 vocabulary terms → clean one-line definitions. Perfect one-to-one card format. |
| **Cap. 11 – Farmacología** | Drug name → class / mechanism / one contraindication. Max doses. Side effects. |
| **Cap. 9 – Microbiología** | Bacteria name → complex → disease. Immunoglobulin → location/function. Virus → transmission route. |
| **Cap. 5 – Anatomía Dental** | Tooth number → root count, eruption date, distinguishing landmark. |
| **Cap. 12 – Bioquímica y Nutrición** | Vitamin → deficiency → oral finding. Textbook flashcard use case. |
| **Cap. 18 – Dolor y Ansiedad** | Injection name → what it anesthetizes. Max dose by drug. |
| **Cap. 20 – Salud Comunitaria** | Index name → what it measures → scale range. Epidemiology term → definition. |
| **Cap. 4 – Anatomía de Cabeza y Cuello** | Nerve branch → area served. Salivary gland → duct name → where it opens. |

## Conceptual Framework Teaching (Instrumentation-Style)

Best for chapters where the content is **a system or a process** — not a list of facts, but a way of thinking. Once the framework is understood, any question within it becomes answerable. Like instrumentation: you don't memorize which instrument for every tooth, you understand the logic and derive the answer.

| Chapter | The Framework |
|---|---|
| **Cap. 6 – Radiología** | Technique errors follow a perfect logical system. One variable changes → one outcome follows → one correction fixes it. Once students see the pattern, they own every variation. |
| **Cap. 14 – Periodoncia** | Staging and grading is an algorithm. Reading a perio chart is a skill. Neither is memorized; both are practiced until the thinking becomes automatic. |
| **Cap. 13 – Biomateriales** | The contraindication table is a decision tree. "Before I use this, I ask these three questions." The logic transfers to new situations. |
| **Cap. 8 – Patología Oral** | Lesion identification follows a framework: location, color, surface texture, can it be wiped off, is it painful, how long has it been there. Same questions every time, different answers. |
| **Cap. 7 – Patología General** | The Stephan curve is a model. Once you understand what's happening at the cellular level, every caries question is just a version of the same story. |
| **Cap. 2 – Histología** | The developmental timeline is a framework. Each stage produces a structure. Each cell makes one thing. Once the map is clear, the questions are easy. |
| **Cap. 16 – Prevención** | The fluoride decision tree. Patient profile → ask three questions → arrive at the right product. Clinical logic, not memorization. |
| **Cap. 3 – Anatomía y Fisiología** | Cellular respiration is a pathway. Draw it once, walk through it, and the questions about where pyruvate goes or what produces ATP become obvious. |

## General Teaching Approaches

### 1. Case-Based Learning (CBL)
One patient touches every chapter simultaneously. A 58-year-old diabetic smoker on warfarin comes in with 6mm pockets and a white lesion on her tongue. Suddenly you're using Ch. 7 (caries process), Ch. 8 (white lesion), Ch. 11 (warfarin), Ch. 14 (perio staging), Ch. 15 (treatment planning), Ch. 19 (medically compromised). This is also exactly what NBDHE Component B tests — patient cases. A well-built case is the highest-leverage teaching tool available.

**Best for:** Cross-chapter integration, exam simulation, Component B prep.

---

### 2. Peer Teaching
The student who explains a concept learns it better than the student who listens. Assign each person a chapter pillar, give them 5 minutes to teach it to the group. The gaps in their explanation reveal the gaps in their understanding immediately.

**Best for:** Any chapter. Especially powerful for the framework chapters (Ch. 14, Ch. 6, Ch. 2) where explaining the logic out loud exposes whether it's truly understood.

---

### 3. Mnemonics
Ordered lists that don't have a natural logic benefit enormously from memory hooks. PDL fiber groups, vitamin-to-deficiency mapping, Angle's classification, the 5 A's of tobacco cessation — these are exactly the kind of content where a phrase or image locks in the order permanently.

**Best for:** Ch. 14 (PDL fibers), Ch. 12 (vitamins), Ch. 16 (5 A's), Ch. 18 (injection sequence), Ch. 9 (red/orange complex).

---

### 4. Error Analysis
Show a wrong answer and ask "what's wrong with this?" It works better than showing the right answer because the brain engages more actively with a mistake.

**Best for:** Ch. 6 (radiographic errors), Ch. 11 (wrong drug choice for a patient), Ch. 22 (what the hygienist did wrong ethically), Ch. 18 (incorrect injection for the procedure).

---

### 5. Diagram Labeling
Blank tooth cross-section. Blank skull diagram. Blank perio chart. Blank Stephan curve with axes only. Students fill them in from memory. Where they go blank is exactly where they need more work. Faster than a quiz and reveals gaps more precisely.

**Best for:** Ch. 2 (tooth anatomy), Ch. 4 (head and neck), Ch. 14 (perio chart), Ch. 7 (Stephan curve), Ch. 6 (landmark identification on X-ray).

---

### 6. Storytelling / Narrative Arc
Turn a disease process into a story told from the patient's perspective or even the bacterium's perspective. "I'm S. mutans. I colonize the pit of tooth #3 after this patient drinks a soda. The pH drops to 5.2. Here's what happens to the enamel next..." Narrative sticks in memory in a way that bullet points don't.

**Best for:** Ch. 7 (caries process), Ch. 8 (oral cancer progression), Ch. 14 (periodontitis development), Ch. 2 (tooth development stages).

---

### 7. Timed Pressure Drills
The exam gives 1–1.5 minutes per question. Practicing under time pressure is a separate skill from knowing the content. Running timed drills where the timer is visible builds the pace judgment that test day requires.

**Best for:** All chapters during final prep. The app's 30-second per-question timer already does this. Especially important for high-question chapters (Ch. 2, Ch. 13) where students may overthink.

---

### 8. Before / After Comparison
Healthy tissue vs diseased tissue — either photos or descriptions. "Here's healthy gingiva. Here's the same area 6 months later." Builds pattern recognition rather than fact retrieval, which is closer to what the exam actually tests.

**Best for:** Ch. 8 (oral pathology), Ch. 6 (radiographic interpretation), Ch. 14 (healthy vs diseased periodontium), Ch. 7 (caries progression stages).

---

### 9. Cross-Chapter Connection Mapping
Draw a web showing how chapters connect. Diabetes alone touches Ch. 12 (nutrition), Ch. 14 (periodontitis), Ch. 19 (special needs), Ch. 11 (pharmacology), Ch. 15 (treatment modification), Ch. 21 (hypoglycemia emergency). Students who only study chapters in isolation miss these connections — the exam exploits them.

**Best for:** End-of-program integration. Draw one map per major systemic condition (diabetes, heart disease, pregnancy, HIV) and show which chapters it activates.

**Key connection maps to build:**
- Diabetes → Ch. 12, 14, 15, 19, 21
- Heart disease → Ch. 11, 15, 18, 19, 22
- Pregnancy → Ch. 6, 12, 16, 18, 19
- HIV/immunocompromised → Ch. 8, 9, 19
- Smoking → Ch. 7, 14, 16, 19
- Xerostomia → Ch. 7, 11, 12, 19

---

### 10. "What Do You Do Next?" Sequential Decisions
Present the first fact of a scenario, ask what they do next, then reveal the result, ask what they do next again. Forces real-time clinical thinking rather than pattern matching to a memorized answer.

**Best for:** Ch. 21 (emergencies), Ch. 15 (treatment planning), Ch. 19 (medically compromised appointments), Ch. 22 (ethical dilemmas that unfold over time).
